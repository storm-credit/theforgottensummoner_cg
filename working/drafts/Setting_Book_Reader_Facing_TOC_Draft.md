# Setting Book Reader-Facing Table of Contents Draft

## Purpose

This draft translates the technical 0-8 setting-book package into a cleaner reader-facing table of contents.

The internal draft package preserves source priority, canon tier, registers, and verification rules. This table of contents points toward the version a reader, collaborator, artist, composer, or adaptation partner could open without needing to understand the audit machinery first.
It is a structure/source guide, not the direct-share manuscript itself.

## Reader-Facing Structure

### Opening: How to Read This World

This opening section explains the promise of `THE FORGOTTEN SUMMONER`.

Focus:

- a human-centered epic fantasy world
- continents shaped by memory, survival, trade, oath, and loss
- heroes and supporting figures who matter because of relationships, not only power
- rumors, relics, places, and names as story engines

Internal source:

- Chapter 0, Canon Policy

Reader-facing treatment:

- Keep this short and confident.
- Do not expose internal state labels.
- Explain that some entries are confirmed, some are uncertain, and some are preserved as rumors.

### Part I. The Five Continents

This part introduces the world through its major continental identities.

Suggested chapters:

1. Ether Continent: central civilization, houses, guilds, holy authority, free cities, arcane institutions, and the Spirit Union.
2. Crimson Continent: desert survival, clan politics, dragon-descended craft, fire memory, and trade hardship.
3. Frost Continent: cold survival, clan order, fortress economy, spirit practice, and supply pressure.
4. Obelisk Continent: collapse memory, seals, exile networks, bone markets, and survival institutions.
5. Oceanic Continent: sea routes, fleets, maritime houses, pirates, temple authority, and undersea rumors.

Internal source:

- Chapter 1, Five Continents

Reader-facing treatment:

- Lead each continent with identity and emotional tone.
- Use social conflicts before tables.
- Keep missing layers as quiet development notes unless the absence itself is interesting.

### Part II. Powers, Orders, and Hidden Systems

This part explains factions without turning the book into an organization chart.

Suggested chapters:

1. Kingdoms, Houses, and Sacred Authority
2. Guilds, Trade Houses, and Market Powers
3. Tribes, Clans, and Survival Bonds
4. Orders, Societies, and Arcane Institutions
5. Underworld Routes and Unofficial Power
6. Exile Networks, Seal Guardians, and Memory Institutions

Internal source:

- Chapter 2, Faction Archive Structure
- Chapter 7, Spatial Map
- Chapter 8, Register Appendix

Reader-facing treatment:

- Explain what each faction lets characters do.
- Avoid overloading readers with root, pressure, or label terminology.
- Use clean display names from the naming chapter.

### Part III. People Worth Seeking

This part separates central heroes from figures who make the world feel inhabited.

Suggested chapters:

1. Named Sages, Artisans, Archivists, and Alchemists
2. Port Masters, Auction Faces, Forgers, Couriers, and Brokers
3. Boundary Figures: When a Supporting Figure May Be a Hero

Internal source:

- Chapter 3, People Worth Seeking
- Chapter 4, Naming Normalization
- Chapter 8, Register Appendix

Reader-facing treatment:

- Keep the main text focused on function, voice, and why someone would seek this person.
- Put verification queues in appendix.
- Do not demote main heroes into background figures for convenience.

### Part IV. Names, Languages, and World Tone

This part explains why names sound the way they do.

Suggested chapters:

1. Display Names and Working Names
2. Continent-Level Naming Flavor
3. Faction Names and Fantasy Tone
4. Protected Name Clusters
5. Legacy Names and Variant Spellings

Internal source:

- Chapter 4, Naming Normalization

Reader-facing treatment:

- Show selected examples, not every correction table.
- Explain name collisions as intentional caution, not bureaucratic clutter.
- If body prose mentions repeated or shared names, explain the reading effect here and keep unresolved identity tracking in appendix.
- Keep modern crime or corporate labels out of polished faction display unless intentionally used.

### Part V. Relics, Gear, Trade Goods, and Things People Want

This part makes the object layer desirable.

Suggested chapters:

1. Why Objects Matter in This World
2. Signature Weapons and Personal Gear
3. Relics, Curses, Costs, and Lost Owners
4. Growth Items and Ego Items
5. Legendary Sets and Collection Treasures
6. Trade Goods, Materials, and Route Economies

Internal source:

- Chapter 5, Items, Relics, and Desire Structure
- Chapter 7, Spatial Map

Reader-facing treatment:

- Start with story desire before stats.
- Include owner, origin, cost, rumor, and map anchor.
- Keep raw candidate lists in appendix until promoted.

### Part VI. Peoples, Bloodlines, Monsters, and Changed States

This part prevents the world from flattening every non-human term into one category.

Suggested chapters:

1. Peoples and Cultures
2. Bloodlines and Mixed Descent
3. Curses, Undead States, and Transformations
4. Monsters, Wild Entities, and Misread Creatures
5. Oceanic and Forest-Folk Questions

Internal source:

- Chapter 6, Species Framework

Reader-facing treatment:

- Use peoplehood evidence before writing full race profiles.
- Keep uncertain groups mysterious but controlled.
- Make monsters useful as ecology, fear, rumor, and scene pressure.

### Part VII. Places, Routes, and Maps That Create Story

This part turns geography into narrative movement.

Suggested chapters:

1. World and Continent Map Principles
2. Cities as Crossroads
3. Ports, Markets, Roads, and Secret Routes
4. Sanctuaries, Ruins, Workshops, and Memory Sites
5. The Crossroad Cities: Visible Map and Hidden Map
6. Travel Costs, Blockades, Rumors, and Supply Lines

Internal source:

- Chapter 7, Spatial Map

Reader-facing treatment:

- Place function comes before visual decoration.
- Routes should carry goods, rumors, danger, and political pressure.
- Do not overdraw unsupported city maps too early.

### Part VIII. Appendix and Production Notes

This part preserves verification without interrupting the reader-facing body.

Suggested appendix sections:

1. Canon and Source Priority Notes
2. Boundary Queue for Hero and Notable Classification
3. Name Variant and Collision Register
4. Item Promotion Register
5. Place and Travel Function Tables
6. Species Classification Guard
7. Rumor, Secret, Reveal, and Foreshadow Controls
8. Source Manifests and Coverage Notes

Internal source:

- Chapter 8, Register Appendix
- Source map
- Assembly index

Reader-facing treatment:

- Keep appendix tables concise.
- Use appendix for uncertainty and production control.
- Do not let appendix language dominate the body voice.

## Recommended Polishing Order

1. Polish the opening and Part I. first because they define the reader's emotional entry.
2. Polish faction and people sections second because they depend on continent framing.
3. Polish naming before finalizing public names across the manuscript.
4. Polish relics, species, and maps after the body voice is stable.
5. Build appendices last, after body prose decides what must be traceable.

## Style Target

The final setting book should feel like an invitation into a living epic fantasy world, not a spreadsheet export.

Target voice:

- clear
- warm
- mythic without being vague
- disciplined without sounding bureaucratic
- useful for writers, artists, composers, and future adaptation work
