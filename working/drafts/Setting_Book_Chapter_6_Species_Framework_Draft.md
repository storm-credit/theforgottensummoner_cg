# Setting Book Chapter 6 Draft: Race, Species, Bloodline, State, and Monster Framework

## Chapter Purpose

This chapter prevents one of the easiest setting-book mistakes: treating every non-human label as a full race, nation, or civilization.

The current material contains signals for species, bloodlines, transformations, monster categories, folklore beings, cursed states, and individual anomalies. They should not be flattened into one encyclopedia list. A half-elf, an undead, a harpy encounter, a dragon-blooded hero, a spiritized person, and a wolf beastfolk tribe are different kinds of world evidence.

The setting book should classify carefully before writing broad lore.

## Classification Buckets

Use five primary buckets.

| Bucket | Meaning | Setting-Book Handling |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Species | A people or biological/cultural group with evidence of social continuity | May receive a people profile if enough social evidence exists. |
| Bloodline | A lineage, mixed ancestry, inherited trait, or family-specific descent | Should be tied to families, inheritance, stigma, power, or identity. |
| State | A condition acquired through magic, curse, death, transformation, spiritization, or corruption | Should not be treated as a natural people unless society evidence exists. |
| Monster | A threat, ecological entity, cursed creature, set-piece enemy, or wilderness category | Should be handled through ecology, rumor, danger, or scene function. |
| Unclear | A term with insufficient evidence for promotion | Track without over-defining. |

The current pass is a classification layer, not final canon naming.

## Promotion Rule

A species profile requires more than one attractive name. It needs evidence of peoplehood.

Useful evidence:

- settlement, homeland, enclave, or migration pattern
- family structure or reproduction signal
- occupation or social role beyond one individual
- political relation to factions
- cultural practice, taboo, belief, craft, or rite
- external prejudice, alliance, trade, conflict, or protection status
- multiple source mentions that point to the same group identity

Without this evidence, keep the term in bloodline, state, monster, or unclear buckets.

## Key Non-Equivalence Rules

The following pairs must not be merged:

- species is not the same as bloodline
- bloodline is not the same as nation
- state is not the same as species
- monster encounter is not the same as peoplehood
- spiritized person is not the same as spirit-born people
- dragon-blooded lineage is not the same as dragon civilization
- cursed creature is not the same as natural race
- continent mention is not the same as continent-wide population

These distinctions protect the setting from accidental overbuilding.

## Spirit Union Guard

The Spirit Union should not be misread as a pure spirit nation. It is better treated as a factional and cultural axis around forest settlements, mobile camps, spirit-related practices, and alliances.

Within that umbrella, multiple identities may coexist:

- humans
- beastfolk
- elves or high elves
- fairy-like signals
- spiritized individuals
- spirit entities
- forest-linked communities

The setting book should avoid writing “the Spirit Union race” as if the faction were a single biological category.

## Ocean Species Guard

Oceanic material contains intriguing signals, especially merfolk and mermaid references. However, the current evidence supports localized contact, representative figures, enclaves, or rumors more safely than a fully defined sovereign ocean civilization.

Allowed current framing:

- coastal or undersea contact
- localized merfolk enclave
- named representative or royal signal
- uncertain sea routes and rumor channels
- future expansion candidate

Avoid for now:

- complete ocean empire
- continent-scale merfolk nation
- fixed political hierarchy without evidence
- assuming all sea monsters are people or all sea people are monsters

## Monster and State Entry Pattern

If a creature or transformed being is not peoplehood evidence, it can still be valuable.

Monster or state entries should answer:

- where it appears
- who fears it
- what rumor surrounds it
- whether it is natural, cursed, summoned, undead, corrupted, or misunderstood
- whether it can speak, trade, negotiate, or remember
- whether it is one entity, a category, or an outbreak
- what story function it enables

This lets the setting book preserve atmosphere without pretending every creature label is a nation.

## Chapter Lock

This chapter locks the species framework for the current setting-book recomposition pass:

- Classify before worldbuilding.
- Do not turn bloodlines, states, or monster encounters into species by default.
- Do not infer continent-wide civilizations from isolated mentions.
- Keep Spirit Union as a factional-cultural umbrella, not a single race.
- Keep ocean species material promising but provisional.
- Use peoplehood evidence before writing full race profiles.
